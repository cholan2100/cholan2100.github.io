Photosynthesis: Doing it right!

Introduction

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy, producing glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) and oxygen (O₂) from carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O). It occurs in two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle).

1. Misrepresentation

Simplified Net Equation:

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

This oversimplified equation in school academia has caused a huge miscommunication of what is happening with Photosynthesis. Teaching usually goes as light energy is used to break the Oxygen O₂ molecules from CO₂, and this O₂ is released into the atmosphere. This is wrong!

Detailed Equation (showing full water use):

12H₂O + 6CO₂ + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ + 6H₂O

It’s the O₂ from H₂O water molecules that gets released out of the plants at the end of photosynthesis. Note: 12 water molecules are split, and 6 are reformed, resulting in a net use of 6 water molecules.

Isotopic Labelling Experiment

In 1941, Ruben and Kamen conducted an experiment using the isotope Oxygen-18 (¹⁸O) to determine the source of oxygen released during photosynthesis. They labeled water (H₂O) with ¹⁸O and carbon dioxide (CO₂) with ¹⁸O separately and observed the oxygen gas (O₂) released by plants.

The results showed that when water containing ¹⁸O was used, the released oxygen gas (O₂) contained ¹⁸O. However, when carbon dioxide containing ¹⁸O was used, the released oxygen gas did not contain ¹⁸O. This conclusively demonstrated that the oxygen released during photosynthesis comes from water (H₂O), not carbon dioxide (CO₂).

Key Insight: The isotopic labeling experiment was pivotal in correcting the misconception about the source of oxygen in photosynthesis.

2. Light-Dependent Reactions

Location: Thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts

Process:

  • Photolysis: Water is split using light energy:
    12H₂O → 24H⁺ + 6O₂ + 24e⁻
  • Electron Transport Chain (ETC): Electrons from water pass through photosystems II and I, generating ATP (via chemiosmosis) and NADPH.

Products:

  • ATP (energy carrier)
  • NADPH (reducing agent)
  • O₂ (released as a byproduct from water)

Key Point: Oxygen released comes solely from water, not CO₂.

3. Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle)

Location: Stroma of chloroplasts

Process:

  • Carbon Fixation: CO₂ is combined with RuBP (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate) by the enzyme RuBisCO to form 3-PGA (3-phosphoglycerate).
  • Reduction: 3-PGA is reduced to G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate) using ATP and NADPH.
  • Regeneration: Some G3P molecules regenerate RuBP, while others form glucose.

Products:

  • Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)
  • Regenerated RuBP (to continue the cycle)

4. Tracking the Atoms

  • Oxygen Atoms: From Water (12H₂O): 12 oxygen atoms are released as 6O₂. From CO₂ (6CO₂): 12 oxygen atoms are incorporated into glucose (6 in C₆H₁₂O₆) and the reformed 6H₂O (6 oxygen atoms).
  • Hydrogen Atoms: 24 hydrogen atoms from 12H₂O are used: 12 in glucose and 12 in the reformed 6H₂O.
  • Carbon Atoms: 6 carbon atoms from 6CO₂ form glucose.

Key Point: "Leftover" hydrogen ions and electrons are used to produce ATP and NADPH; oxygen from CO₂ not in glucose ends up in reformed water.

5. Energy Flow

  • Light Energy: Captured by chlorophyll in the light-dependent reactions.
  • Chemical Energy: Stored as ATP and NADPH.
  • Glucose Production: ATP and NADPH power the Calvin cycle to convert CO₂ into glucose.

6. Summary of Key Concepts

  • Oxygen Source: Released O₂ comes only from water (H₂O), not CO₂.
  • Water Use: 12H₂O are split, but 6H₂O are reformed, resulting in a net use of 6H₂O.
  • Internal Use: Hydrogen ions and electrons from water produce ATP and NADPH; oxygen from CO₂ not in glucose is in reformed water.
  • Energy Transformation: Light energy is converted into chemical energy (glucose) through a two-stage process.
